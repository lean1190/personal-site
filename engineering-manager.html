Communication For Engineering Managers

Taking care of a team is damn hard, and it is even harder when we cannot express effectively our expectations, our values, and our beliefs.
During my time as a team lead, I learned how to tailor my communication for different personalities, resolved conflicts, had difficult conversations, and practiced the most effective ways to align a team around goals.
Let me share a story with you.

How it started

When I started working as a software engineering team lead, I was panicking in excitement. It was the first time I was in such a role, I suddenly had to take care of people instead of code. I had no clue what I was doing, and I knew I wanted to do so many things, but I didn't know where to start.

I was still taking care of my software engineer's tasks, and I was totally swamped with responsibilities and lack of time. On top of this, there was a team member that was underperforming. I never dealt with that situation before, and I didn't know what to do about it. I reached out to fellow managers looking for inspiration, and knowledge. They suggested I give the underperforming team member the benefit of the doubt before making a statement I would regret. I took their advice, and I did exactly the opposite.

I messed up hard. I had a 1:1 where I messed with his confidence, and his value to the team.
This team member pushed me to learn several management lessons in a couple of hours:

# Lesson 1: Assume nothing
As a manager, my perception of him might've been accurate, but that didn't mean I didn't need to listen to his point of view before making up my mind. Maybe I was missing a part of the story that I didn't let him tell me about.

# Lesson 2: My words can unleash chaos, frustration, and stress
The way I delivered my feedback was unexpected, hostile, and unempathetic. A recipe for disaster. I was not aware of how much impact my feedback had on that person's life.

# Lesson 3: We are here to solve this together
I had no plan, ideas, or even a suggestion on how to address this behavior. I shouldn't have put all the responsibility of dealing with the situation on his shoulders. This only created more frustration for both of us.

Fortunately, it was not too late. After that slap on my face, we had another 1:1, this time in completely different terms. I was prepared, mentally and practically. I apologized for my behavior in the previous meeting, acknowledged my fuck ups, and suggested we looked at the situation from a different angle. I had ideas on how to address the behavior, and I made sure he knew I was gonna help him go through the steps to get there. The changes were radically positive, and the person never had a relapse in performance again.

Sounds familiar?

The engineering manager and team lead roles can be a rollercoaster of emotions, a never-ending line of internal and external conflicts, and a full house of self-doubt. But it can also be the most fulfilling, and enriching journey you can go through.
